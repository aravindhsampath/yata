# YATA — Yet Another Todo App

**A todo app built for how ADHD brains actually work.**

---

## Why YATA?

Most todo apps are built for organized people who need a place to write things down. YATA is built for people who *know* what they need to do but struggle with the doing — the prioritizing, the starting, the not-forgetting, the not-getting-overwhelmed.

YATA doesn't hide your work from you. It doesn't let you pretend tasks don't exist. It puts everything on your plate, right in front of you, and gives you just enough structure to start chipping away.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Three Lanes

YATA uses three color-coded lanes instead of traditional priorities:

- **Green — Do Today.** These are the tasks you're tackling right now. Today's workload. The stuff that needs to move.

- **Yellow — Aim for This Week.** Important, but not burning. You'll get to these when today's greens are handled. They're visible so you don't forget, but they're not pressuring you.

- **Red — Interesting, But Wait.** Things you want to do eventually, but only after greens and yellows are clear. Parking these here prevents them from hijacking your focus with their novelty. ADHD brains chase dopamine — red lane is where shiny distractions go to wait their turn.

This is not a priority system. It's an **urgency system** designed to protect your focus. Green is "now", yellow is "soon", red is "later — and that's okay."

### Rollover: Your Todo List Tells the Truth

At midnight, every undone task rolls forward to today. No exceptions.

This means your "today" view is always your **real** workload. Not what you planned last Tuesday. Not an optimistic fantasy. The actual pile of things waiting for you.

**The pile is the feature.** When it grows, you feel it. That visual weight is intentional — it creates healthy pressure to triage, to finish, to reschedule, or to let go. ADHD brains need external cues because internal motivation is unreliable. The pile is your external cue.

Items are never silently moved to a backlog. They're never collapsed behind a "3 items from yesterday" disclosure. They're right there, in your face, because pretending they don't exist is how tasks get lost forever.

### Dates Are "When It Lands on Your Plate"

When you schedule a task for next Wednesday, you're not setting a due date. You're saying "this lands on my plate on Wednesday." When Wednesday arrives, it joins the pile.

Future dates show you what's coming. Today shows your status quo. You can look ahead to plan, but the only view that matters is today — because that's where you do the work.

### Repeating Tasks Materialize Automatically

Set up a rule once in the Repeating tab — "change car tires" twice a year, "take out recycling" every Tuesday, "review budget" monthly. YATA creates occurrences on the right dates automatically.

When a repeating task appears in your today view, it's just a regular todo item. Mark it done, delete it if it's not relevant this time, or let it roll over if you don't get to it. The rule keeps running independently.

You manipulate occurrences from Home. You manipulate rules from Repeating. Simple separation.

### Reschedule, Don't Ignore

If a task rolled over but today isn't the right day for it, reschedule it. Pick a future date and it moves there. This is an honest act — you're not deleting it, you're deliberately choosing when to face it.

This is healthier than letting tasks pile up without intention. Rescheduling forces a micro-decision: "when will I actually do this?" That's more useful than the task sitting in a growing pile while you avoid eye contact with it.

### Done Is Done, Not Deleted

Completed tasks are never deleted. They live in the "Recently Done" section — a visible record of what you've accomplished. For ADHD brains, seeing completed work counteracts the feeling that you never get anything done.

The size of the recently done list is configurable in Settings. Show the last 10, 25, 50 — whatever keeps you motivated without cluttering the view.

## Design Philosophy

### Ultra-Minimal, Instant Launch

YATA loads instantly. There's no onboarding, no account creation, no tutorial. Open it and start adding tasks. Every millisecond of friction is an opportunity for an ADHD brain to get distracted.

### Information Density Without Clutter

Everything fits on one screen. The week strip, three urgency lanes, and done section are all visible without scrolling deep. Pill-shaped items are compact but readable. No cards, no expanded views, no nested screens for basic operations.

### Tactile and Responsive

- Tap a pill to mark it done
- Swipe left to delete
- Drag to reorder within and across lanes
- Haptic feedback on every interaction

Every action gives immediate physical feedback. No confirmation dialogs for routine operations. The app respects that you meant what you did.

### Local-First, Optionally Connected

YATA works fully offline with no account required. Your data lives on your device. For users who want more — syncing across devices, external API access, web dashboard — an optional self-hosted API connection is available. But the core experience never requires internet.

## Who YATA Is For

- People with ADHD who need their todo list to be honest with them
- People who've tried Todoist, Things, Reminders, and found them either too complex or too easy to ignore
- People who want to see their real workload, not a curated version of it
- People who value speed and simplicity over features and customization

## Who YATA Is Not For

- People who need project management (subtasks, dependencies, team assignment)
- People who need extensive categorization (tags, contexts, areas)
- People who want their todo app to also be their calendar, notes app, and habit tracker
- People who are already organized and just need a checklist

## The Name

YATA — Yet Another Todo App. Because the world doesn't need another todo app. But it might need *this* one.
